Finding 1: Discounts and promo codes aren't buying us loyalty.
We compared customers who used a discount/promo code against those who paid full price. If discounts were working the way we hope — building loyal, higher-spending customers — we'd expect the discount group to spend more, come back more often, and be happier with their purchase.
They don't. Both groups spend about the same (~$59-60 on average), have made roughly the same number of past purchases (~25), and rate their experience almost identically (3.74 vs 3.76 out of 5). We checked this across every loyalty level too — even our most loyal customers show no difference in spending whether or not they used a discount. That means we're giving away discounts to people who would likely have bought anyway.

Finding 2: How customers get their order has a small link to satisfaction.
We looked at whether delivery method affects how happy customers are. Customers who pick up in-store or pay for Next Day Air are somewhat more likely to leave a low rating (about 1 in 4) compared to customers using standard shipping (about 1 in 5).
This is a smaller, more modest finding — not a five-alarm fire, but worth a mention. One possible read: customers paying for speed, or picking up in person, may simply have higher expectations, and any small hiccup (wait time, store experience) disappoints them more than it would a patient "standard shipping" customer.

We tested two operational factors — shipping type and payment method — against customer satisfaction. Payment method showed no meaningful pattern. Shipping type showed a modest effect: Store Pickup and Next Day Air customers were somewhat more likely to leave a low rating (~24-26%) than Standard shipping customers (~19%), though the underlying average ratings differ by less than half a point. This is a minor, secondary finding — not a major driver of dissatisfaction in this dataset.

--seasonality & geography
-- revenue by season
Unlike typical apparel retail patterns, this dataset shows no meaningful seasonality — total revenue and category mix remain stable across Fall, Winter, Spring, and Summer, including in categories (Outerwear) where strong seasonal demand would normally be expected. This suggests either a geographically diverse customer base evening out regional seasonal effects, a business model less exposed to weather-driven demand than typical apparel retailers, or — worth flagging as a caveat — that "Season" may reflect a labeling/catalog attribute rather than actual purchase timing.

--top 10 states by revenue
Order counts range from 81 to 96, revenue from $5,172 to $5,784 — a tiny spread (~12%) across the top 10 states out of 50. With 3,900 customers spread across 50 states, that's an average of 78 orders per state — and every state you see here is sitting within ~15-20 orders of that average.

Purchase volume is nearly uniform across all 50 states (~78 orders each, ±20%), with no meaningful regional concentration. This pattern — combined with the lack of seasonal variation observed earlier — suggests Location and Season in this dataset may not reflect genuine customer geographic/temporal distribution, but rather a synthetic or evenly-sampled dataset construction. Geographic and seasonal targeting recommendations should be treated with caution given this limitation.

At risk customer definition
A customer gets flagged "at-risk" only if all three of these are true at once:
1. They've bought a lot from us historically (top 50% of customers by past purchase count the "High" or "Very High" loyalty tier)
2. But they don't shop often anymore (their buying frequency is Quarterly or Annually — not Weekly/Monthly like an active customer)
3. And they weren't happy with their experience (their review rating falls in the bottom 25% of all ratings)

So as per the data, A loyal customer who used to shop with us regularly and spent well — but their last couple of purchases weren't great experiences, and we haven't seen them in months. That's exactly the kind of customer we'd want our best salesperson to personally call before they're gone for good — not someone who already left, but someone showing the warning signs. That's what this segment is: 180 customers, worth about $10,400, fitting that exact profile — mostly men, mostly buying Clothing, rating us 2.6-3.0 out of 5, and not coming back often.
